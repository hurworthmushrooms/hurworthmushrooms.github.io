Welcome to Hurworth Mushrooms

A zero-waste, organic mushroom project

A love of all things fungi, and a kaupapa that involves innovative management of our natural ecosystems, are core to our beliefs.
Our farm has developed onto a low-footprint, highly efficient converter of wood biomass into food. We use natural systems and energy sources because we belive in working as closely in with nature as possible. Our mushrooms are grown as closely as possible to the way they would grow in the wild. We dont use any insect, fungal or herbicide on our crops. Our mission is to move into other areas of industry including: foresty, market gardens, mycoremediation and alternatives to animal protein.
Hopefully, we'll bring about a healthy backyard, town, province and perhaps, country.

Mushrooms & Health

Immune System

Mushrooms (more so "wild" mushrooms like oyster mushrooms), stimulate your immune system to make antibodies. This is in reaction to very large beta-glucan molecules that kick-start our metabolism.

Vitamin D

Dried mushrooms, when exposed to the sun, make huge amounts of Vitamin D. Button mushrooms will generally have less Vitamin D than wild mushrooms as they are grown in the dark.

Microbiome

Your microbiome is the bacteria in your gut. In the wild, mushrooms control bacteria and are more similar to us than plants so eating them promotes a balanced microbiome

Protein Source

Mushrooms and mycelium products are an excellent source of protein for non-meat eaters. Dried mushrooms are about 90 percent protein.

Mushrooms Demystified

A mushroom is a fruit

What we call a "mushroom" is actually the seed-dispersal part of a much larger organism that is composed of thousands of very thin threads, called mycelium.

Mushrooms are not plants

Mushrooms have their own kingdom, like ourselves, in the Animal Kingdom and plants in the the Plant Kingdom, mushrooms are in the Fungi Kingdom.

  • More

Mushrooms grow from spores

A spore is much like a seed of a plant. When two comapatible spores germinate they grow into mycelium, threads that grow through the substrate.

  • More

Mushrooms Make Soil

In the wild, mushrooms are vital to the recycling of nutrients. Mushrooms convert dead plant matter into digestible compounds that eventually become soil in which more plants can grow in.
